You are planning a surprise birthday party for the child of your househelp, and you have to design everything from the very beginning.

You start by writing down how many friends you want to invite. Each time the number grows big, you bundle ten friends into one special "team" and see how many full teams and how many single friends you have left. Then, you give each friend a fun number name.

Then you plan how to decorate the room by choosing your own balloon pattern—maybe grouping them in 2s, 5s, or 10s—You can draw it in your notebook too.

You also choose how big the birthday cake should be and think about whether the number of slices will be enough for all the friends you invited.

Next, you pack the return gifts. You arrange the gifts in straight rows of ten and see if any gifts are left over at the end.

Finally, you organise the cups, plates, and chocolates. Instead of counting them one by one, you stack them in tens and then place the extra ones beside them, just like a part supply shopkeeper.

Are you ready to plan a wonderful surprise party for your friend?

Share with us how you felt organising a party for your househelp child.

On a Sunday morning, you saw your mother organise your cupboard. What is the first thing you should do? You should help your mother organise your cupboard. Right?

First, you take out everything from the cupboard and spread it neatly on the floor or on the bed. You look carefully at each item. You group similar things together — all the books in one place, all the clothes in another, all the toys in another and all the socks together. Then, you count the items in each group. Maybe you find 14 books, 9 toys, 15 clothes and 5 pairs of socks. (Just an example). You compare the groups and write/stick the picture which group has more, which has less and which has the same number.

Next, you decide where each group should go. You keep big and heavy items on the lower shelves and light and small items on the upper shelves. You arrange your school books in ascending order and your clothes in the descending order of size from the biggest to the smallest. Try noticing the place value of the number of books too.

Now it is time to fix your socks. You pair them in groups of two and find out if the total number of socks is even or odd. If one sock is left without a pair, you know the number is odd. If all socks are paired, the number is even.

Then you move to your toys. You arrange them on the shelf and make number bonds of 10. For example, if you keep 6 toys in one box, how many more are needed to make 10? You add some more toys and see:  $ 6 + 4 = 10 $,  $ 7 + 3 = 10 $,  $ 5 + 5 = 10 $. You are using addition without even realising it!

Finally, you make up a small story about how you spent Sunday morning arranging your cupboard with the help of your mother!

Do you feel proud of yourself as you used sorting, comparing, counting, addition, number bonds, place value and ordering to make your cupboard beautiful.
